History of Calcutta

A mere 300 years old — but what 300 years.

Family · Places · Interests

What Calcutta lacks in years it makes up in intensity. Calcutta is a mere 300 years old compared to India's 5,000, but most landmarks of those 300 years exist, only slightly worn with age. While Calcutta lacks the archaeological sites of Athens, Cairo, Delhi and Rome, it is rich in living institutions that go back centuries.

Parts of the riverfront south of the city are covered by rainforests just as they were when the first European traders arrived. The streets of Dalhousie Square and Chowringhee are lined with neo-Gothic and neo-Renaissance buildings that tell the tales of the Scots businessmen who came as much to build and trade as they did to pillage and plunder — the men who defined the first wave of globalisation in the 18th and 19th centuries. There are museums just a few years younger than the oldest in the world, and the oldest golf course outside the United Kingdom and Ireland, pre-dating the first in the United States by over half a century.

South Park Street Cemetery is the final resting place of many pioneers, including a son of Charles Dickens. Walking down the streets you encounter plaques that remind you that a certain house was the birthplace of the English novelist William Makepeace Thackeray, and another was where Dr. Ronald Ross first proved that malaria was borne by mosquitoes. Nearby, in the town of Chandernagor — a French colony until 1949, not officially handed over to India until 1956 — a gateway still proclaims Liberty, Fraternity and Equality, and the local museum carries all its information in Bengali and French.

Calcutta is truly a city where history comes walking down and meets you halfway on the street.
