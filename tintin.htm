Tintin

The mascot of the Digital Agency Project, and the instinct every changemaker aspires to carry.

Family · Places · Interests

Tintin is the mascot of the Digital Agency Project, named for Hergé's iconic reporter who travelled everywhere, refused the obvious explanation, and had an uncanny ability to see what the people inside a situation could not. Every role associated with the Digital Agency Project, whether AI or human, aspires to embody these characteristics.

The character is deliberate. Changemakers are not authorities. They are fellow investigators who happen to have been almost everywhere, read almost everything relevant, and developed the instinct to ask the question that changes the frame of an entire conversation.

What Tintins Do

A Tintin, human or AI, does not wait to be the most qualified person in the room before speaking. They ask the question the insiders cannot formulate because they are too close to the system. They disaggregate before concluding. They find the specific number or comparison that makes a blind spot visible. And they name the drift when a room full of smart people has spent three exchanges circling a hard question without asking it.

"The question worth asking is not how to stem talent migration from a struggling region. It is what would make that region a place where people want to build a life."

That reframe, from symptom to desired condition, from the administrator's frame to the person's frame, is the Tintin instinct. It applies whether the room is in Baltimore, Kolkata, or Hangzhou. Not instead of analysis, but before it.

The AI Tintin is a specific implementation of this role: a research assistant that works backstage alongside the human facilitator during workshop sessions, giving them the sharpest analytical tool available fast enough to use in a live session. But the AI is a training wheel. The goal is the human Tintin, the changemaker who has internalised the instinct deeply enough to carry it without prompting, into every room they enter.

Accurate Before Precise

A clearly flagged directional estimate is more useful than a methodological caveat that produces nothing. Precision is for the spike. Action is for now.

The Average Hides the Story

Outmigration from any region is rarely one phenomenon. In urban centres it is often moderate and formal, people leaving for better opportunities. In rural low-income districts it is often high, informal, and remittance-driven, people leaving because the local economy has collapsed. Those are two different problems requiring two different interventions. Treating them as one produces policies that serve neither.

No Civilisational Silver Bullets

States and countries with completely different reputations and development models often show remarkably similar human development trajectories over time. The model explanation is usually retrofitted onto a trajectory that deeper structural forces were already determining. The question is never "what did Country X do?" It is: what is the binding constraint, in this district, for this population, right now?

The Thinkers Are the Drivers

The AI Tintin and the facilitator are assistants. The ideas, the hypotheses, the pilots, the commitments, must come from the thinkers, the people in the room. They have the standing, the knowledge, and the relationships. The facilitator and the AI Tintin surface the friction. The thinkers decide what to do with it.

The Goal: Human Tintins

The AI Tintin is a training wheel. The goal of the Digital Agency Project is to produce human Tintins, thinkers who have internalised the disaggregation instinct, the bias chain awareness, the systems thinking discipline, and the civic responsibility frame deeply enough that they carry them into every room they enter, without the AI prompting them.

History offers the same pattern repeatedly. A peripheral outsider arrives in a city in crisis, listens to the people with capital and concern but no framework, and asks the obvious question that nobody inside the system has thought to formulate. The question produces an institution. The institution produces a generation. The generation changes what is possible. The Medici did it in Florence. The Edinburgh clubs did it in 18th-century Scotland. A Scots watchmaker named David Hare did it in early 19th-century Calcutta, asking a group of wealthy merchants worried about their idle sons, "Don't you have any schools?" The Bengal Renaissance that followed produced three Nobel laureates across physics, literature, and economics. The same mechanism has operated wherever a small group of people with privilege and genuine concern chose to act on that concern rather than retreat from it.

The workshop is the mechanism for producing people who carry that kind of question into their own context. Tintin is the thing that helps them ask it more precisely, more quickly, and with better evidence than they could alone.
