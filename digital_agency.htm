The Problem

How might we sustain the energy that results in lasting positive impact from the desire for such change from a cohort of well-meaning, resourceful individuals and organisations?

In 1992, I joined CMC Limited in Calcutta straight out of engineering college. CMC was doing something unusual for an Indian IT company at the time — it was using technology to solve problems that actually mattered in India. I worked on automating the steelmaking process at Bhilai Steel Plant, one of the largest steel factories in the world. The work was hard, the problems were real, and the impact was visible.

Then the outsourcing boom happened.

By the mid-1990s, the Indian IT industry had found a more lucrative model: do the chores that companies in high-income countries didn't want to do, at a fraction of the cost. It worked spectacularly, by certain measures. The engineers who could work on problems worth solving aren't. They're busy.

This isn't a moral failing. It's a structural one. The labor arbitrage model that built India's IT industry also ensured that its meritocratic elite became, in practice, an extension of high-income country economies. They are aware that problems exist around them. When they gather socially, they talk about them. But the conversation usually ends with "population," "corruption," or a convenient someone else to blame. Nobody leaves with a plan.

I don't think this is because they don't care. I think it's because they've never been given a structure that matches their skills to the problems in front of them.

The Medicis, the Tagores, the Edinburgh intellectuals who between them produced modern economics, modern medicine and modern philosophy in about fifty years — they faced versions of the same problems. They didn't wait for better conditions. They built something small enough to start and consequential enough to matter, and brought others along as they went. Crucially, they didn't stop at charity. They intervened in the systems themselves.

Over 33 years in software engineering, I worked with human-centered design, agile development, systems engineering and DevOps to build products that solved real organisational problems. At some point it became obvious that the same structure could be pointed at wicked problems — not just software backlogs, but actual interventions in broken systems. The Digital Agency Project is that structure, applied.

Geography

Middle-income societies broadly — with initial focus on the US Mid-Atlantic, South Asia and Southeast Asia, where proximity and familiarity create the conditions for relationships rather than transactions.

Where We Are

The Digital Agency Project is at an early stage. The workshop is being piloted now. The marketplace is in design. What we are building toward — the frameworks, the tools, the relationships, the body of writing — will take shape in the coming years. We are not in a hurry. Lasting change rarely is.
